  ### What to Look For
 When you’re shopping on a budget, the first thing to kill is the idea that “economical” equals “bad.” It doesn’t. But you do have to be ruthless about what you actually need. Here’s the short list of what separates a good buy from a regret:

 
- **Materials that survive washing.** Stainless steel, borosilicate glass, silicone, and cast iron are your friends. Avoid thin aluminum or plastic that warps in the dishwasher.

- **Simple mechanics.** Fewer buttons = fewer things to break. A manual frother or a fundamental thermometer will outlast any digital gadget with a proprietary battery.

- **Real-world capacity.** A 4-quart pot sounds fine until you try to boil pasta for two. Check the usable volume, not just the advertised size.

- **Warranty or return policy.** If a seller won’t stand behind a item, that’s a red flag. Stick with brands that offer at least 30 days.

 One more thing: ignore “professional grade” labels on sub-gear. That’s marketing, not engineering.

  ### What to Avoid on a Budget
 I’ve seen too many people buy “budget-friendly” items that end up costing more in replacements. Here’s what to skip:

 
- **Nonstick pans under ** They scratch, peel, and end up in the trash within a year. Buy stainless or cast iron instead.

- **Plastic cutting boards.** They warp in the dishwasher and harbor bacteria in knife grooves. Bamboo or rubber are better.

- **Multi-function gadgets.** A tool that claims to chop, slice, spiralize, and juice usually does none of those well. Buy dedicated tools.

- **Inexpensive knife sets.** A single chef’s knife will outperform a block set. Avoid sets entirely.
