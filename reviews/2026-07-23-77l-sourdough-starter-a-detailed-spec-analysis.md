Title: 77L Sourdough Starter Jar 2-Pack — Wide-Mouth Jars with Useful Accessories, but Marketing Overpromises If you're maintaining a sourdough starter, you already know the basics: you need a jar that's uncomplicated to clean, lets you track temperature and feeding schedules, and seals well enough to store in the fridge without leaking. This two-pack from 77L tries to cover all of that in one kit. The question is whether the accessories actually help, or if you're paying for things you could rig yourself with a rubber band and a fridge magnet thermometer. Based on the specs provided, here's what this kit actually offers.  The core value here's the jar itself. At 35 oz each, two jars give you enough volume to maintain a starter and have a backup fermenting batch going simultaneously. The specs show that wide mouth is a genuine practical improvement over narrow-mouth mason jars — you can actually reach inside with a sponge or brush, which matters when dried starter residue gets stubborn. The acacia wood lids are thick and solid, and the product claims they maintain a superior seal even during long refrigeration. If you have ever had a loose lid leak starter slurry all over your fridge shelf, that seal matters. Looking at the data, accessories are a mixed bag. The time-marked elastic band and temperature sticker are simple tools that work as advertised: you mark your last feeding time on the band, stick the thermometer on the jar wall, and you have a visual reference for both variables. that's genuinely useful for beginners who forget when they fed last or who are learning how temperature affects fermentation speed. What the specifications tell us is that the scraper is a entry-level silicone spatula — nothing special, but it gets the job done. The doubled fermentation holes in the lid are where the marketing starts to drift. The product describes these as creating "the excellent conditions for dough fermentation" and enabling a "one-step starter" process. that's overblown. Fermentation holes allow gas exchange, which is standard for any sourdough jar lid. Having two holes instead of one is marginally better for airflow, but it isn't a transformative characteristic. You are still going to need to feed your starter regularly and manage temperature — no lid design shortcuts that. What the specs don't tell you: there's no information on the glass thickness, whether the jars are dishwasher-protected, or the exact dimensions of the jar opening. For a product centered on ease of cleaning, knowing whether these can go on the top rack of a dishwasher would be useful. Based on the, based on our review available information, product also doesn't specify the material of the jar body — "large tins" in the features suggests metal, but the description clearly describes glass jars for sourdough. That inconsistency is worth noting. Based on the data provided, I'd assume glass, but the wording is sloppy. Another gap: no mention of the cloth cover material or whether it's reusable. If you're using this for long, cold-proof ferments in the fridge, a breathable cloth cover is fine. But if you want to store the starter for a week without feeding, you will need the solid wood lid for a proper seal. Summary: This kit is a reasonable entry point for someone starting their sourdough journey or wanting a dedicated jar unboxing without piecing together accessories. The wide-mouth design and included band/thermometer are genuinely useful. The fermentation holes and "one-step" claims are marketing fluff. If you already have a system that works, this is not a must-buy. Who this is for: This makes sense if you're modern to maintaining a sourdough starter and want everything in one box — the jar, a lid that seals, a band to track feedings, and a thermometer to learn how temperature affects your starter. It also works if you maintain two separate starters (e.g., a white flour and a whole wheat) and want matching jars. Who should look elsewhere: If you already have a reliable mason jar system and a sharpie for marking feeding times, you do not need this. The accessories are convenient but not key. Also, if you prefer light plastic or silicone lids for easier handling, the acacia wood adds weight and requires hand-washing.
