# Ollygrin Semi-Automatic Espresso Machine with Grinder — PID Temperature Control and 19 Grind Settings, but Missing Some Key Specs If you're the type who weighs your coffee beans, times your shots, and adjusts grind size until the extraction hits that sweet spot between 25 and 30 seconds, you've probably been eyeing a machine that combines grinding and brewing in one unit. The question is whether the Ollygrin Semi-Automatic Espresso Machine with Grinder delivers the control you need without cutting corners you'll notice later. The headline features are promising on paper. A built-in conical burr grinder with 19 settings, PID temperature control holding within ±0.5°C, and a touchscreen that shows real-time pressure and water temp. That's a spec list that puts this in the conversation for home baristas who want more than a pod machine but aren't ready for a commercial setup.

 The 20-bar pressure is standard for this class of espresso machine. Most home units run between 15 and 20 bar, and the extra headroom means you're less likely to stall the pump with a fine grind. In practice, that 20 bar gets you consistent pressure through the puck, which is what actually matters for extraction. The PID temperature control is the real differentiator here. Holding within half a degree Celsius means your water temperature won't drift during a shot or between shots. That directly affects flavor — sour shots usually come from water that's too cool, bitter ones from water that's too hot. If you're pulling back-to-back shots for yourself and someone else, the PID keeps the second shot tasting like the first. The specs show that built-in grinder with 19 settings covers the range from fine enough for espresso to coarse enough for pour-over, though you'll likely spend most of your time in the finer settings. The self-cleaning mode is a practical touch — it shakes out retained grounds automatically, which means less disassembly and fewer stale grinds mixing into tomorrow's shot. The sealed bean hopper with the rubber ring is worth noting. Coffee beans lose volatile oils and aromatics quickly when exposed to air. A sealed hopper slows that degradation, so you're not refilling every other day or wasting beans that have gone stale before you grind them. What's missing from the specs — and it's a meaningful gap — is any mention of boiler type, heat-up time, or water reservoir capacity. For a machine with PID, you'd want to know whether it uses a single boiler, a dual boiler, or a thermoblock. That determines how fast you can steam milk after pulling a shot. The product listing says a milk frother is included in the box, but without knowing the steam system, you're guessing at workflow. Similarly, no dimensions or weight are listed, which makes it hard to tell whether this fits under your cabinets or on a cramped counter. The touchscreen showing real-time pressure and temperature is useful for dialing in, but it's also a potential failure point compared to mechanical buttons. If the screen goes, you lose control of the machine. That's a risk worth considering if you expect this to last several years. Who this is for: Someone who wants a single appliance for grinding and brewing espresso, values temperature stability for consistent shots, and is willing to learn the 19 grind settings and dose control. It suits a home user who drinks espresso-based drinks regularly and wants to improve their technique without buying separate grinder and espresso machine. If you're the person who adjusts grind size between beans and keeps a notebook of shot parameters, this machine's PID and grind adjustment range make sense. Who should look elsewhere: If you steam milk for lattes and cappuccinos frequently, the lack of boiler specs means you're gambling on steam performance. For someone who wants to push a button and get coffee without thinking about grind size or dose weight, this machine has too many adjustments to be effortless to use.
