 ### What to Look For
 Furniture shopping is about trade-offs. You can have solid wood, but it’ll cost you. You can have something portable and easy to move, but it won’t survive a move. Looking at the data, trick is knowing which trade-offs matter for your situation.

 ### Materials That Matter

- **Solid wood** – Heavy, rugged, repairable. Look for oak, maple, or walnut. Pine is softer but cheaper. Avoid “solid wood” claims if the piece uses veneer over MDF.

- **Plywood** – Better than particleboard. It’s layered and resists warping. Good for shelves and desks.

- **MDF (medium-density fiberboard)** – Dense, smooth, but heavy. Prone to swelling if it gets wet. Fine for pieces that won’t move.

- **Particleboard** – Light, cheap, crumbles under stress. Avoid for anything that needs to hold weight or survive a move.

 ### Joinery and Construction

- **Dovetail or mortise-and-tenon joints** – Gold standard. Indicates quality.

- **Dowels or screws** – Acceptable for mid-range furniture.

- **Cam locks and dowels** – Standard in flat-pack furniture. Fine for budget items, but don’t expect them to survive disassembly.

- **Staples and glue** – Red flag. Means the piece is built to last one season.

 ### Finish and Hardware

- Look for **UV-cured lacquer or polyurethane** finishes. What the specifications tell us is that the resist scratches and spills.

- Drawer slides should be **ball-bearing or full-extension**. Avoid plastic slides.

- Hinges should be **soft-close** if you value quiet. Not key, but pleasant.

 ### Features to Consider
 Not every characteristic is worth paying for. Here’s what actually matters based on how you’ll use the piece.

 ### Size and Fit

- Measure your space **before** you shop. Include doorways and hallways.

- Leave at least 24 inches of walking space around furniture.

- For sofas: seat depth should be 20–22 inches for average comfort. Deeper is for lounging.

- For desks: at least 30 inches wide for a monitor and keyboard. 48 inches is better for dual monitors.

 ### Assembly and Portability

- Flat-pack furniture saves money but costs time. Expect 1–3 hours for a desk or wardrobe.

- Pre-assembled furniture costs more but is ready immediately. Money well spent if you hate tools.

- If you move often, avoid heavy solid-wood pieces. Look for modular designs that can be taken apart.

 ### Multi-Functionality

- Storage ottomans, sofa beds, and nesting tables save space.

- But multi-function often means compromise. A sofa bed is rarely as user-friendly as a dedicated sofa or bed.

- Ask yourself: will I actually use the extra function? If not, skip it.

 ### Weight Capacity

- Check the spec sheet for weight limits on shelves, drawers, and seats.

- A typical bookshelf should hold 30–40 lbs* (based on available data) per shelf. A desk should hold at least 50 lbs.

- If the spec doesn’t list it, the manufacturer probably doesn’t want you to know.

 ### Final Thoughts
 Great value furniture doesn’t have to be high-end, but it does have Honestly, about what it is. A desk made of particleboard is fine if you know it’s temporary. A sofa that uses economical foam will sag in a year — that’s bad value. The comes from understanding your own calls for:

 
- **If you move often**: uncomplicated to carry, modular, flat-pack. Accept lower durability.

- **If you stay put**: solid wood or plywood, quality joinery, good finish.

- **If you’re on a tight budget**: look for used solid-wood furniture. In terms of everyday use, it’s often cheaper than latest particleboard and lasts decades.

- **If you want convenience**: pre-assembled, mid-range materials. You pay for the labor, but you save your weekend.

 One last thing: ignore “lifetime warranty” claims. The furniture industry defines “lifetime” as the expected life of the product, not yours. A 5-year warranty is meaningful. A 1-year warranty means they know it’ll break.
