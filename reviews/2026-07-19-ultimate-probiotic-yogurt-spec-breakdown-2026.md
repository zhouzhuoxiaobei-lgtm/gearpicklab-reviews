What the specifications tell us is that the claim about making “trillions of live probiotics” is a marketing statement, not a spec. There’s no lab data provided to verify bacterial counts. The actual yield depends on your starter culture, incubation time, and temperature consistency—not just the machine. Who this is for This makes sense if you regularly make large batches of yogurt and want individual servings in glass jars. The 12-jar setup is simple to use for flavor customization per jar (add fruit, honey, or extracts to each before incubation). The adjustable temperature and long run time also suit people who experiment with different probiotic strains that require specific conditions. If you already have a starter culture for L. reuteri or L. gasseri and want to scale up production, the capacity and control are directly useful. Who should look elsewhere If you only make yogurt occasionally or for one or two people, 12 jars is overkill—you’ll end up with more yogurt than you can use before it spoils. Also, if you need precise temperature accuracy for finicky cultures and the listing doesn’t confirm digital control or stability, a unit with a published accuracy spec might be a safer bet. The missing power and dimensions also mean you’ll have to gamble on fit and energy use.
