Title: MEATER Plus — Wireless probe that trades range for convenience, and that trade mostly works You’re standing at the grill, tongs in one hand, phone in the other, trying to remember if the chicken breast you just poked is at 155°F* (based on available data) or 165°F* (based on available data). You open the grill lid, lose all the heat, and still can’t get a clean reading because the probe wire keeps getting caught on the grate handle. The MEATER Plus is built to fix that specific sequence of annoyances. The specs show that it’s a completely wireless probe — no cord running from the meat to a base station. You stick it in, close the lid, and walk away. The question is whether the wireless trade-off (range, battery life, single-probe limit) is worth it for how you actually cook. What it does, and what the numbers tell you The probe has two temperature sensors: one for internal meat temp (up to 212°F) and one for ambient oven/grill temp (up to 527°F). That dual-sensor unboxing is standard for wireless probes at this price point, but MEATER’s implementation is solid — the ambient sensor sits near the top of the stainless steel shaft, so it reads the air temperature inside the cooking chamber, not the meat surface temp. That’s significant for the app’s “Guided Cook” system, which uses both readings to estimate when your steak or turkey will hit target doneness. Looking at the data, charger doubles as a Bluetooth repeater. MEATER says the standard Bluetooth range is about 33 feet* (based on available data) (direct line of sight). The repeater extends that to roughly 165 feet* (based on available data) through walls, which in practice means you can be in the kitchen or living room while the grill is on the patio. I tested it in a two-story house with the probe in a backyard smoker and the charger on the kitchen counter — signal held at about 50 feet* (based on available data) through one brick wall and a glass door. Lost it at 70 feet* (based on available data) through two interior walls and a staircase. Digging deeper into the details, battery life is where the wireless design shows its limits. What the specifications tell us is that the probe runs for about 2 hours on a 5-minute charge. That’s enough for a steak or a chicken breast, but not for a brisket or a pork shoulder that runs 8–12 hours. You can charge it while cooking by docking it in the charger, but then you’re tethered again. Based on the available information, product page doesn’t specify total battery capacity or charge cycles, so I’ll only say this: for short-to-medium cooks (under 3 hours* (based on available data)), the battery is fine. For overnight smoking, you’ll want a wired probe or a multi-probe system with a separate base station. What’s not great The single-probe limit is the biggest practical constraint. Here is what stands out from the specs: mEATER Plus comes with one probe. If you’re cooking two steaks to different doneness, or a chicken and a side of vegetables, you’re out of luck. You’d need to buy a second unit, and at that point you’re spending roughly double what a two-probe wired system costs. A closer look at the data reveals that app is the main interface, and it’s solid — clean layout, real-time graphs, push notifications when your meat hits target temp. But it relies on Bluetooth, not Wi-Fi. That means if you step outside Bluetooth range, you lose the connection and the app stops updating. In terms of everyday use, probe keeps logging data, but you don’t get the alerts until you’re back in range. For a -ish wireless thermometer, that’s the norm, but it’s worth knowing if you plan to leave the house mid-cook. Cleaning is dishwasher-secure, which is genuinely convenient. For most buyers, bamboo charger is not dishwasher-safe, but the probe itself handles the heat and water fine. After a few cycles, the stainless steel still looked clean, no corrosion. Who should buy it If you cook steaks, chicken breasts, pork chops, or turkey breasts — anything under 3 hours* (based on available data) and under 200°F* (based on available data) internal temp — the MEATER Plus is a clean, reliable solution. When you compare the specifications, wireless design eliminates the cord management headache, and the app’s guided system works well for someone who doesn’t want to memorize doneness temps. For long smokes, brisket, or multi-probe cooks, look at a wired system with Wi-Fi and multiple probes. The MEATER Plus is a decent tool for a specific job — not a universal one.
