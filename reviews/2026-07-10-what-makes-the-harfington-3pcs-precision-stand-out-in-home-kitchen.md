The specs show that hARFINGTON set gives you three brass sliding calipers in 80mm, 100mm, and 120mm lengths. That covers most space-saving-scale measuring you’d do around a workbench or jewelry station. Brass is a reasonable choice here — it’s denser than aluminum, resists corrosion better than steel in humid environments, and the material has enough heft to feel solid in hand without being heavy enough to weigh down a tool roll. What you’re actually getting These are minimal double-scale calipers with a sliding jaw and a straight end. The markings are engraved into the brass, which means they won’t rub off like printed scales on cheaper plastic rulers. The listing says the markings are “clear,” and based on the product images, they appear to be etched with decent depth. You get both metric and imperial scales on each caliper, which is useful if you’re switching between systems — common in jewelry work where wire gauges are often in inches but bead sizes are in millimeters. The sliding mechanism is basic. There’s no lock screw, no fine-adjustment wheel, no digital readout. You push the jaw against the object, hold it, and read the scale. To expand on that point, that’s fine for rough measurements or quick checks. If you need repeatable precision within 0.1mm* (based on available data) — say, for machining or tight-fit assembly — these aren’t the tool. Brass expands more than steel with temperature changes, and without a locking mechanism, the jaw can shift when you lift the caliper off the part. Where they fall short The biggest limitation is the lack of any locking or fine-adjustment characteristic. On a digital caliper, you press a button to zero it and lock the jaw. On these, you hold the jaw in place by friction alone. If you’re measuring something small like a 2mm bead hole, it’s easy to bump the jaw and lose the reading. The straight-end design also means you can’t measure inside diameters of deep holes — the jaw is flat, not hooked. Looking at the data, product description doesn’t specify the graduation increments. Based on the images, the metric scale appears to be 1mm increments, which is typical for this style of caliper. That means you’re estimating tenths of a millimeter by eye. For jewelry making, that’s often acceptable. For precision engineering, it’s not. Who should buy these If you work with beads, rings, small wood pieces, or glass components and need a quick, portable measurement tool that doesn’t require batteries or calibration, this set is practical. What the specifications tell us is that the three sizes let you pick the right length for the job — the 80mm is handy for space-saving rings, the 120mm for longer beads or spacers. They’re also inexpensive enough that losing one in a workshop drawer isn’t a crisis. Skip these if you need repeatable measurements within 0.1mm* (based on available data), if you’re measuring inside diameters of deep holes, or if you want a tool that can zero out and subtract thickness. For those tasks, a basic digital caliper with a lock screw will cost more but deliver actual precision. For pocket-friendly, no-fuss measuring of small objects, the HARFINGTON set does what it says — just don’t expect more than the price implies.
