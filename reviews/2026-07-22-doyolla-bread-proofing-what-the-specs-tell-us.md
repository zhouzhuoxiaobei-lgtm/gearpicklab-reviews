Title: DOYOLLA Bread Proofing Baskets Set — Solid rattan baskets with a full tool kit, but sizing details are vague If you’re getting into sourdough and want to move past shaping dough on a countertop, a proofing basket (banneton) is one of those tools that actually makes a difference. It supports the dough’s structure during the final rise and leaves those spiral marks on the crust that signal “artisan loaf.” This DOYOLLA set gives you two baskets—one oval, one round—plus a handful of accessories, all for a price that undercuts buying each piece separately. The question is whether the baskets themselves hold up, and whether the extras are useful or just filler. The specs show that baskets are made from natural rattan, which is the standard material for bannetons. Rattan wicks moisture away from the dough’s surface, helping form a dry skin that leads to better oven spring and a crispier crust. The product listing says the rattan complies with EU food standards—that’s a reasonable assurance against unwanted chemicals in contact with your dough, though no specific certification number is provided. Looking at the data, liners included with each basket mean you can also proof high-hydration doughs (70% and above) without worrying about sticking, which is a practical touch for wetter sourdough recipes. The size situation is where things get less clear. The listing says “10 inch Oval & 8.5 inch Round,” then adds “For the Size Detail, please check 6th image.” That’s not perfect—you shouldn’t have to hunt through product photos to find dimensions. Based on what’s stated, the 8.5-inch round basket will comfortably fit a standard 500g* (based on available data) loaf (about 1 pound of dough). The 10-inch oval is better for batard-shaped loaves or larger 750g* (based on available data)–1000g* (based on available data) bakes. If you mostly bake round boules, the round basket will be your primary tool, and the oval will see less use. What the specifications tell us is that the included accessories are a mixed bag. You get a metal bench scraper (useful for dividing dough and cleaning the work surface), a dough whisk (handy for mixing wet doughs without overworking them), five bread lames (the razor-like tools for scoring loaves before baking), a silicone basting brush, and a plastic brush for cleaning the baskets. The lames are a genuine value-add—scoring blades dull quickly, and having spares saves you from buying replacements separately. Based on the available information, silicone brush and plastic brush feel like filler; you likely already have something similar in your kitchen. The dough whisk is a pleasant bonus if you don’t own one, but it’s not essential. One limitation: the product doesn’t specify the weight of the baskets or the thickness of the rattan. Heavier, denser rattan holds its shape better over time and resists cracking. Without that data, it’s hard to judge long-term durability. The care instructions say “hand wash with warm water and keep dry after clean,” which is standard for rattan—never soak them, and let them air dry fully between uses to prevent mold. Summary This set suits beginner to intermediate sourdough bakers who want two basket sizes and a handful of useful extras in one purchase. The natural rattan construction and included liners cover the basics well. The vague sizing details and lack of build thickness data mean you’re trusting the brand’s reputation rather than hard specs. If you already own a banneton and just need a spare, buying a single basket might be simpler. But for someone starting out or upgrading from cloth-lined bowls, the DOYOLLA set provides everything needed to proof, score, and shape bread without buying tools individually. Who this is for This is for home bakers who bake 1–2 loaves per week and want the convenience of having both round and oval baskets ready. If you’re experimenting with different dough hydration levels or loaf shapes, the two sizes give you flexibility without buying multiple sets. The included lames and scraper mean you won’t need to hunt down scoring tools separately. Who should look elsewhere If you bake exclusively high-hydration doughs (80% and above) and prefer unlined baskets for maximum crust texture, the included liners may get in the way—you’d be better off with a single unlined banneton.
