 ### What to Look For
 Before you buy anything, ask yourself three questions:

 
- **Will I actually use this weekly?** A sous-vide machine is cool. A blender you use three times a year is a waste of counter space.

- **Can I clean it in under five minutes?** If a gadget has seventeen crevices and a “hand wash only” label, you will stop using it.

- **Does it do one thing well?** Multi-function devices often do everything poorly. A dedicated rice cooker beats a “12-in-1” pressure cooker for plain rice every time.

 For beginners, I recommend starting with a cooktop, a petite appliance for quick meals, a milk frother (if you drink coffee), and a solid ventilation solution. Here’s what the data tells us about each category.

That gives you faster boiling times and better temperature control than a standard coil burner.

**What to check:**

- **Power output:** Look for at least 1500W* (based on available data) for decent boiling. What the specifications tell us is that the avanti model doesn’t list wattage in the product sheet, but most portable induction cooktops in this class sit around 1300-1800W* (based on available data). If the listing doesn’t specify, assume it’s on the lower end.

- **Pan compatibility:** Induction requires magnetic pans. If you own stainless steel or cast iron, you’re fine. If you have aluminum or glass pans, they won’t work. The product listing does not mention a pan test, so you’ll need to check your existing cookware with a magnet.

- **Controls:** Look for physical buttons or knobs over touch panels. Touch panels on budget cooktops are often slow and frustrating. Based on the available information, avanti uses a digital display with buttons — that’s acceptable, but not as tactile as a knob.

 **Verdict:** A portable induction cooktop is a capable first buy if you don’t have a dependable stove. Just make sure your pans work with it. The Avanti is a reasonable entry point, but don’t expect restaurant-level precision.
